Major Experiences

Nebraska Tractor Test Lab

At the Nebraska Tractor Test Lab I developed foundational skills in tractor diagnostics, equipment testing, and data collection. This hands-on work with agricultural machinery built the technical foundation that I have carried into every role since.

CLAAS Internship

During my CLAAS internship, I strengthened the diagnostic and testing skills I developed at the Nebraska Tractor Test Lab while being exposed to the global agricultural equipment industry. I traveled to multiple locations and collaborated with engineers and technicians from around the world, which expanded my understanding of global equipment standards and workflows. The experience deepened my knowledge of forage harvesting equipment — particularly silage choppers — and provided insight into the design, testing, and performance expectations of modern agricultural machinery.

Scheideler Farms

I've always helped out on the farm as much as I could because I know the work I do now will contribute to my future. From working cattle and managing pastures to maintaining equipment, the farm is where my work ethic and practical skills were shaped.

El Salvador Tractor Training Workshop

Role & Instruction: I served as the lead technician and instructor, training operators on the use of a John Deere 6155M tractor and a 7100 series finger-style planter. Working through a translator, I delivered operational and maintenance instruction to more than 20 participants. I was even able to share technical insights with local John Deere mechanics who had limited experience with this newer equipment.

Organizations & Collaboration: The workshop was coordinated by Catholic Relief Services with support from a Warren Buffett Foundation initiative. I was invited to participate through Synergy Ag (Montana) and Jack Alexander. The experience reinforced the value of partnerships between nonprofit organizations, private industry, and agricultural professionals.

Challenges & Adaptation: The language barrier presented an initial challenge, requiring patience, clear demonstrations, and adaptation in communication. Building trust also took time, as the operators were being asked to consider new practices after years of working in established ways. Recognizing that these methods supported their livelihoods helped me approach the training with respect and empathy.

Leadership & Personal Growth: This experience was a defining moment in my development. Being trusted to lead instruction in an unfamiliar environment pushed me beyond my comfort zone and strengthened my confidence as a leader. The gratitude and resilience shown by the participants broadened my perspective on global agriculture and reinforced the importance of practical education and knowledge sharing.

New Zealand Study Abroad

Agricultural Practices Observed: Traveled all over the South Island, touring different farms — from dairy operations to cow-calf and many sheep producers.

Differences from U.S. Farming: The U.S. values its farmers and wants them to succeed; the New Zealand government places significant regulations on farmers, making it nearly impossible to have a small farm without another income source like tourism. Another major difference: no feedlots. Baby calves from dairies are veal or considered a waste and expensive to deal with because they lack the infrastructure or feed to raise them for meat.

Sustainability Practices: New Zealand is focused on grass-fed, no-till farming, and integrating polyculture systems.

Cultural Lessons: The trip reinforced that people in agriculture are very similar across the world. It was an eye-opening experience being in a country that imports most of the food it eats.

What Broadened My Perspective: Traveling to another country really opened my eyes to new possibilities, and I decided I wanted to travel much more.
